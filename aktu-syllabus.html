Module 1: Relativistic Mechanics

Welcome to Relativistic Mechanics, where classical Newtonian laws break down, and Einstein's paradigm shifts take over. This module covers the theoretical foundations of high-velocity phenomena, introducing the realization that space and time are interconnected.

1.1 Frames of Reference

A frame of reference provides a coordinate system to define the position and motion of an object. The study of relativity classifies them into two distinct types based on their acceleration state:

  • Inertial Frames: Unaccelerated frames where Newton’s First Law holds true. Observers in these frames move with uniform translational velocity relative to each other. Calculations in Special Relativity are exclusively valid between inertial frames.
  • Non-Inertial Frames: Accelerated or rotating frames where fictitious or 'pseudo' forces (like centrifugal or Coriolis force) must be empirically introduced to satisfy Newton's laws of motion. Earth is technically a non-inertial frame due to its rotation, but for small-scale experiments, it is approximated as inertial.

1.2 Michelson-Morley Experiment

Conducted in 1887 by Albert A. Michelson and Edward W. Morley, this famous experiment sought to detect the relative motion of matter through the stationary "luminiferous aether"—a hypothetical medium thought to be necessary for the propagation of light waves.

Setup: A beam of light was split into two orthogonal paths using a half-silvered mirror. After traveling equal distances, the beams reflected back and recombined to form an interference pattern. The apparatus was rotated by 90 degrees to observe any fringe shift caused by the Earth's motion through the aether.

Result: The null result demonstrated a fringe shift of exactly zero (within experimental error). This proved that the speed of light is completely independent of the observer's motion or the earth's motion, dealing a fatal blow to the aether theory and laying the groundwork for Einstein's postulates.

1.3 Lorentz Transformations

The Galilean transformations assume absolute time ($t = t'$) and absolute space. At velocities approaching the speed of light ($c$), these classical transformations yield incorrect results (e.g., velocities exceeding $c$). This warranted the new Lorentz Transformations, grounded on Einstein's two postulates of Special Relativity:

  1. Principle of Relativity: The fundamental laws of physics are identical in all inertial frames of reference.
  2. Constancy of Speed of Light: The speed of light in a vacuum ($c$) is absolute and constant for all observers, regardless of the motion of the light source or the observer.

Transformation Equations:

Consider an inertial frame $S'$ moving with velocity $v$ in the positive x-direction relative to a stationary frame $S$. According to the constraints $x^2 + y^2 + z^2 = (ct)^2$ and $x'^2 + y'^2 + z'^2 = (ct')^2$, the coordinates relate by:

$$ x' = \frac{x - vt}{\sqrt{1 - v^2/c^2}} = \gamma(x - vt) $$ $$ y' = y \quad \text{and} \quad z' = z $$ $$ t' = \frac{t - vx/c^2}{\sqrt{1 - v^2/c^2}} = \gamma\left(t - \frac{vx}{c^2}\right) $$

Where $\gamma = \left(1 - \frac{v^2}{c^2}\right)^{-1/2}$ is the Lorentz Factor. Inverse transformations are obtained by replacing $v$ with $-v$ and swapping primed/unprimed variables.

1.4 Consequences: Length Contraction & Time Dilation

Length Contraction: An object measuring proper length $L_0$ in its rest frame will be measured as shorter by an observer moving relative to it. The contraction only happens along the direction of relative motion.

$$ L = L_0 \sqrt{1 - \frac{v^2}{c^2}} = \frac{L_0}{\gamma} $$

Time Dilation: A moving clock ticks more slowly than a clock at rest. If the proper time interval measured by a rest clock is $\Delta t_0$, a stationary observer measuring the moving clock will record a dilated time $\Delta t$.

$$ \Delta t = \gamma \Delta t_0 = \frac{\Delta t_0}{\sqrt{1 - \frac{v^2}{c^2}}} $$

1.5 Addition of Velocities

Classically, velocities simply add ($u = u' + v$). Relativistically, if an object moves at velocity $u'$ in frame $S'$, and $S'$ moves at $v$ relative to $S$, the velocity $u$ measured in $S$ is:

$$ u = \frac{u' + v}{1 + \frac{u'v}{c^2}} $$

If $u' = c$, then $u = \frac{c + v}{1 + (cv/c^2)} = \frac{c(1 + v/c)}{1 + v/c} = c$. The speed of light cannot be exceeded.

1.6 Mass-Energy Equivalence ($E=mc^2$)

Mass is fundamentally a concentrated form of energy. As a particle accelerates, its relativistic mass increases via $m = m_0\gamma$. The work done to accelerate it manifests as kinetic energy ($K$).

Derivation:

Kinetic energy $K$ is the integral of the rate of work done: $ dK = v \cdot dp $ where momentum $p = mv$.

Using $m = \frac{m_0}{\sqrt{1 - v^2/c^2}} \implies m^2 (c^2 - v^2) = m_0^2 c^2$. Differentiating yields: $c^2(2m dm) - (m^2 2v dv + v^2 2m dm) = 0 \implies c^2 dm = mv dv + v^2 dm$.

Notice that $v \cdot dp = v \cdot d(mv) = v(m dv + v dm) = mv dv + v^2 dm$. Therefore, $v \cdot dp = c^2 dm$.

$$ \int dK = \int_{m_0}^{m} c^2 dm \implies K = (m - m_0)c^2 $$

Total Energy $E = K + \text{Rest Energy}(m_0c^2) = (m - m_0)c^2 + m_0c^2 = mc^2$.

Momentum-Energy Relation: $E^2 = p^2c^2 + (m_0c^2)^2$.

PYQ 1 [AKTU 2022-23]: Postulates of Special Relativity

Question: State the fundamental postulates of the special theory of relativity. Show that $x^2+y^2+z^2-c^2t^2$ remains invariant under Lorentz transformation.

Solution:

1. Principle of Relativity: All laws of physics are the same in all inertial reference frames.
2. Constancy of Speed of Light: The speed of light in vacuum is absolute ($c$) for all observers.

Invariance proof: Need to prove $x'^2+y'^2+z'^2-c^2t'^2 = x^2+y^2+z^2-c^2t^2$. Substitute Lorentz equations: $x' = \gamma(x-vt)$, $y'=y$, $z'=z$, $t' = \gamma(t-vx/c^2)$.

$$ L.H.S = \gamma^2(x-vt)^2 + y^2 + z^2 - c^2\gamma^2(t-vx/c^2)^2 $$ $$ = \gamma^2 [x^2 - 2xvt + v^2t^2 - c^2(t^2 - 2vxt/c^2 + v^2x^2/c^4)] + y^2 + z^2 $$ $$ = \gamma^2 [x^2 + v^2t^2 - c^2t^2 - v^2x^2/c^2] + y^2 + z^2 $$ $$ = \gamma^2 [x^2(1 - v^2/c^2) - c^2t^2(1 - v^2/c^2)] + y^2 + z^2 $$

Since $\gamma^2 = \frac{1}{1-v^2/c^2}$, it cancels out the $(1-v^2/c^2)$ terms entirely.

$$ = x^2 - c^2t^2 + y^2 + z^2 = R.H.S.\quad \text{(Hence Proved)} $$

Module 2: Electromagnetic Field Theory

This module unites electric and magnetic phenomena into a singular continuum using the tools of vector calculus, culminating in the elegant Maxwell's Equations which govern all classical electrodynamics.

2.1 Coulomb’s Law in Differential Form

The classical Coulomb force equation evaluates discrete point charges. In continuous charge distributions, we express the electric field divergence locally using Gauss's Law.

Derivation:

Integral form of Gauss's Law: $ \oint_S \vec{E} \cdot d\vec{a} = \frac{Q_{enc}}{\epsilon_0} $

The enclosed charge $Q_{enc}$ is the volume integral of the volume charge density $\rho$: $ Q_{enc} = \int_V \rho \, d\tau $

By the Divergence Theorem, any surface integral of a vector field over a closed surface converts to a volume integral of its divergence: $ \oint_S \vec{E} \cdot d\vec{a} = \int_V (\nabla \cdot \vec{E}) \, d\tau $

$$ \int_V (\nabla \cdot \vec{E}) \, d\tau = \frac{1}{\epsilon_0} \int_V \rho \, d\tau $$

Since this holds for any arbitrary volume, the integrands must be equal. This gives the differential form of Gauss's Law (Coulomb's Law):

$$ \nabla \cdot \vec{E} = \frac{\rho}{\epsilon_0} $$

Significance: Electric field lines originate from positive charges ($\nabla \cdot \vec{E} > 0$) and terminate on negative charges ($\nabla \cdot \vec{E} < 0$).

2.2 Equation of Continuity

The equation of continuity is the localized mathematical formulation of the Principle of Conservation of Electric Charge. Charge can neither be created nor destroyed; if charge decreases in a volume, it must flow out as current.

Current $I$ flowing out of a closed surface $S$ is the rate of decrease of charge within bounding volume $V$:

$$ I = \oint_S \vec{J} \cdot d\vec{a} = -\frac{dQ}{dt} = -\frac{d}{dt} \int_V \rho \, d\tau $$

Applying the Divergence Theorem on the current density $\vec{J}$: $ \oint_S \vec{J} \cdot d\vec{a} = \int_V (\nabla \cdot \vec{J}) \, d\tau $

$$ \int_V (\nabla \cdot \vec{J}) \, d\tau = \int_V \left(-\frac{\partial \rho}{\partial t}\right) d\tau $$ $$ \nabla \cdot \vec{J} + \frac{\partial \rho}{\partial t} = 0 $$

For steady currents (where charge density doesn't change over time, $\partial \rho / \partial t = 0$), the equation simplifies to $\nabla \cdot \vec{J} = 0$.

2.3 Maxwell's Equations

James Clerk Maxwell encapsulated all classical electrodynamics into four fundamental differential equations. We formulate their differential forms in vacuum/free space:

  1. Gauss's Law for Electricity: $ \nabla \cdot \vec{E} = \frac{\rho}{\epsilon_0} $
    (Electric charges act as sources/sinks producing diverging electric fields.)
  2. Gauss's Law for Magnetism: $ \nabla \cdot \vec{B} = 0 $
    (Magnetic monopoles do not exist; magnetic field lines always form continuous closed loops.)
  3. Faraday's Law of Induction: $ \nabla \times \vec{E} = -\frac{\partial \vec{B}}{\partial t} $
    (A time-varying magnetic field induces a circulating (curling) non-conservative electric field.)
  4. Ampère-Maxwell Law: $ \nabla \times \vec{B} = \mu_0 \vec{J} + \mu_0 \epsilon_0 \frac{\partial \vec{E}}{\partial t} $
    (Magnetic fields are generated by both conduction currents $\vec{J}$ and displacement currents $\epsilon_0 \frac{\partial \vec{E}}{\partial t}$.)

2.4 Poynting Theorem and Poynting Vector

The Poynting Vector $\vec{S} = \vec{E} \times \vec{H}$ represents the directional energy flux density (rate of energy transfer per unit area) of an electromagnetic field.

Poynting Theorem Statement: The rate at which work is done by the electromagnetic field on charges within a volume $V$ is equal to the rate of decrease of electromagnetic energy stored in the volume, minus the rate at which energy flows out through the bounding surface $S$.

$$ \int_V (\vec{J} \cdot \vec{E}) d\tau = -\frac{d}{dt} \int_V \left( \frac{\epsilon_0 E^2}{2} + \frac{B^2}{2\mu_0} \right) d\tau - \oint_S (\vec{E} \times \vec{H}) \cdot d\vec{a} $$

Which in differential form is: $ \frac{\partial u}{\partial t} + \nabla \cdot \vec{S} + \vec{J} \cdot \vec{E} = 0 $

PYQ 1 [AKTU 2022-23]: Maxwell's Displacement Current

Question: Explain the concept of displacement current. How did Maxwell modify Ampere's circuital law?

Solution:

Classical Ampere's law $\nabla \times \vec{B} = \mu_0 \vec{J}$ proved inconsistent with the equation of continuity. Taking divergence of both sides gives $\nabla \cdot (\nabla \times \vec{B}) = \mu_0 (\nabla \cdot \vec{J}) = 0$. But continuity demands $\nabla \cdot \vec{J} = -\partial \rho / \partial t$, which isn't always zero (e.g., during capacitor charging).

Maxwell postulated that a changing electric field also acts as a current. From Gauss's law, $\rho = \epsilon_0 (\nabla \cdot \vec{E})$. Thus, $\nabla \cdot \vec{J} = -\partial / \partial t (\epsilon_0 \nabla \cdot \vec{E}) = -\nabla \cdot (\epsilon_0 \partial \vec{E} / \partial t)$.

Therefore, $\nabla \cdot (\vec{J} + \epsilon_0 \frac{\partial \vec{E}}{\partial t}) = 0$. The total current density $\vec{J}_{total} = \vec{J}_c + \vec{J}_d$, where $\vec{J}_d = \epsilon_0 \frac{\partial \vec{E}}{\partial t}$ is the displacement current density.

Modified Ampere's Law: $ \nabla \times \vec{B} = \mu_0 \vec{J} + \mu_0 \epsilon_0 \frac{\partial \vec{E}}{\partial t} $.

PYQ 2 [AKTU 2021-22]: Speed of EM Waves

Question: Show that electromagnetic waves travel with the speed of light in vacuum using Maxwell's equations.

Solution:

In vacuum (free space), charge density $\rho = 0$ and current density $\vec{J} = 0$.

Take the curl of Faraday's Law: $ \nabla \times (\nabla \times \vec{E}) = \nabla \times \left(-\frac{\partial \vec{B}}{\partial t}\right) = -\frac{\partial}{\partial t}(\nabla \times \vec{B}) $.

Vector Identity: $ \nabla \times (\nabla \times \vec{E}) = \nabla(\nabla \cdot \vec{E}) - \nabla^2 \vec{E} $. Because $\nabla \cdot \vec{E} = 0$, the LHS is $-\nabla^2 \vec{E}$.

Using Ampère-Maxwell Law for the RHS: $\nabla \times \vec{B} = \mu_0 \epsilon_0 \frac{\partial \vec{E}}{\partial t}$.

$$ -\nabla^2 \vec{E} = -\frac{\partial}{\partial t}\left( \mu_0 \epsilon_0 \frac{\partial \vec{E}}{\partial t} \right) \implies \nabla^2 \vec{E} = \mu_0 \epsilon_0 \frac{\partial^2 \vec{E}}{\partial t^2} $$

Compare to the standard 3D wave equation $\nabla^2 \Psi = \frac{1}{v^2}\frac{\partial^2 \Psi}{\partial t^2}$. The velocity of the EM wave is $v = \frac{1}{\sqrt{\mu_0\epsilon_0}} \approx 3 \times 10^8 \text{ m/s} = c$.

PYQ 3 [AKTU 2020-21]: Transverse Nature of EM Waves

Question: Prove that electromagnetic waves in free space are transverse in nature.

Solution:

Assume a plane EM wave traveling in the +z direction. The fields $\vec{E}$ and $\vec{B}$ depend only on $z$ and $t$, meaning partial derivatives with respect to $x$ and $y$ are zero ($\frac{\partial}{\partial x} = 0, \frac{\partial}{\partial y} = 0$).

From Gauss's Law in free space: $\nabla \cdot \vec{E} = 0 \implies \frac{\partial E_x}{\partial x} + \frac{\partial E_y}{\partial y} + \frac{\partial E_z}{\partial z} = 0$.

Since the $x$ and $y$ derivatives are zero, $\frac{\partial E_z}{\partial z} = 0$. The longitudinal component $E_z$ is constant in space. Since EM waves are oscillatory, a constant component means no wave. Hence, $E_z = 0$.

Similarly, from $\nabla \cdot \vec{B} = 0$, we find $B_z = 0$. Since the wave propagates along $z$, and field variations only occur in the $x$-$y$ plane, the fields $\vec{E}$ and $\vec{B}$ are strictly perpendicular to the direction of propagation. Thus, they are transverse.

PYQ 4 [AKTU 2019-20]: Poynting Theorem Derivation

Question: State and prove Poynting's Theorem mathematically.

Solution:

Start with the identity: $\nabla \cdot (\vec{E} \times \vec{H}) = \vec{H} \cdot (\nabla \times \vec{E}) - \vec{E} \cdot (\nabla \times \vec{H})$.

Substitute Maxwell's equations: $\nabla \times \vec{E} = -\frac{\partial \vec{B}}{\partial t}$ and $\nabla \times \vec{H} = \vec{J} + \frac{\partial \vec{D}}{\partial t}$.

$$ \nabla \cdot (\vec{E} \times \vec{H}) = \vec{H} \cdot \left(-\frac{\partial \vec{B}}{\partial t}\right) - \vec{E} \cdot \left(\vec{J} + \frac{\partial \vec{D}}{\partial t}\right) $$

Using $\vec{B} = \mu_0 \vec{H}$ and $\vec{D} = \epsilon_0 \vec{E}$, note that $\vec{H} \cdot \frac{\partial \vec{H}}{\partial t} = \frac{1}{2}\frac{\partial (H^2)}{\partial t}$.

$$ \nabla \cdot (\vec{E} \times \vec{H}) = -\frac{\partial}{\partial t}\left( \frac{1}{2}\mu_0 H^2 + \frac{1}{2}\epsilon_0 E^2 \right) - \vec{J} \cdot \vec{E} $$

Integrating over volume $V$ and applying the divergence theorem on the left side yields the Poynting theorem (refer to section 2.4). The term $\vec{J} \cdot \vec{E}$ signifies ohmic power dissipation within the volume.

Question: Define characteristic impedance of free space. Show that its value is approximately $377 \, \Omega$.

PYQ 8 [AKTU 2015-16]: Energy Density of EM Wave

Question: Prove that in a plane electromagnetic wave, the average energy density of the electric field equals the average energy density of the magnetic field.

Solution:

Energy density of E-field: $u_E = \frac{1}{2} \epsilon_0 E^2$. Energy density of B-field: $u_B = \frac{1}{2\mu_0} B^2$.

Using the relation $E = cB$ and $c^2 = \frac{1}{\mu_0 \epsilon_0}$:

$$ u_E = \frac{1}{2} \epsilon_0 (cB)^2 = \frac{1}{2} \epsilon_0 c^2 B^2 = \frac{1}{2} \epsilon_0 \left(\frac{1}{\mu_0 \epsilon_0}\right) B^2 = \frac{1}{2\mu_0} B^2 = u_B $$

Therefore, $u_E = u_B$. The energy is equally shared between the electric and magnetic fields.

Solution:

The electrostatic field is conservative as $\nabla \times \vec{E} = 0$, meaning it can be expressed as the negative gradient of a scalar potential $V$. Thus, $\vec{E} = -\nabla V$.

Substitute this into Gauss's Law:

$$ \nabla \cdot (-\nabla V) = \frac{\rho}{\epsilon_0} \implies \nabla^2 V = -\frac{\rho}{\epsilon_0} $$

This is Poisson's Equation. In a charge-free region ($\rho = 0$), it reduces to Laplace's Equation:

$$ \nabla^2 V = 0 $$

Module 3: Quantum Mechanics

Quantum Mechanics introduces the dual nature of matter and radiation, replacing deterministic classical trajectories with probabilistic wavefunctions that govern phenomena at the atomic and subatomic scales.

3.1 Black Body Radiation & Planck's Hypothesis

A black body is an idealized physical body that perfectly absorbs all incident electromagnetic radiation, regardless of frequency or angle of incidence. Classical physics attempted to explain the spectral energy distribution using the Rayleigh-Jeans Law, which unfortunately predicted the "ultraviolet catastrophe"—an infinite energy emission at progressively shorter wavelengths.

In 1900, Max Planck resolved this catastrophe by theorizing that the atomic oscillators in the cavity walls cannot emit or absorb energy continuously. Instead, energy is quantized in discrete packets (quanta):

$$ E = nh\nu $$

Where $n$ is an integer ($n=1,2,3...$), $h = 6.626 \times 10^{-34} \text{ J s}$ is Planck's constant, and $\nu$ is the oscillator frequency. Planck's Radiation Law perfectly matched experimental curves at all wavelengths.

3.2 Compton Effect

Discovered by Arthur H. Compton in 1923, this effect describes the inelastic scattering of X-rays or gamma rays off weakly bound electrons. The scattered radiation features a structurally longer wavelength than the incident radiation. This phenomenon decisively confirmed the particle nature of light—that photons behave as distinct corpuscles carrying quantified momentum $p = h/\lambda$ and energy $E = hc/\lambda$, capable of undergoing relativistic elastic collisions with particles.

Compton Shift Formula Derivation Notes: Applying relativistic conservation of energy and conservation of momentum in 2D to a photon-electron collision yields the shift in wavelength ($\Delta \lambda$):

$$ \Delta \lambda = \lambda' - \lambda = \frac{h}{m_0 c} (1 - \cos\theta) $$

Where $\frac{h}{m_0 c} = 0.0243 \text{ \AA}$ (or $2.43 \text{ pm}$) is defined as the Compton wavelength of the electron, and $\theta$ is the photon scattering angle relative to its initial trajectory.

3.3 Wave-Particle Duality (de Broglie Hypothesis)

If light waves exhibit particle properties (as per the Compton and Photoelectric effects), Louis de Broglie hypothesized symmetrically that physical particles of matter (like electrons) must possess wave-like properties. The de Broglie wavelength $\lambda$ for a particle of rest mass $m$ and velocity $v$ (non-relativistic momentum $p$) is:

$$ \lambda = \frac{h}{p} = \frac{h}{\sqrt{2mK}} = \frac{h}{\sqrt{2mqV}} $$

Where $K$ is kinetic energy and $V$ is the accelerating potential for a charged particle $q$. This was empirically validated by the Davisson-Germer electron diffraction experiment.

3.4 Schrödinger's Wave Equation

Erwin Schrödinger formulated the foundational differential equation defining the quantum state evolution of a physical system, characterized by the wavefunction $\Psi$.

Time-Independent Schrödinger Equation Derivation:

Consider a 1D matter wave propagating along the x-axis: $\Psi(x,t) = \psi(x) e^{-i\omega t}$. The classical standard wave equation gives spatial second derivative: $\frac{\partial^2 \Psi}{\partial x^2} = -k^2 \Psi$.

Using the de Broglie relation $p = \hbar k \implies k^2 = p^2/\hbar^2$ (where $\hbar = h/2\pi$).

Also, Total Energy $E = \text{Kinetic} + \text{Potential} = \frac{p^2}{2m} + V(x) \implies p^2 = 2m(E-V)$.

Substitute $k^2 = \frac{2m(E-V)}{\hbar^2}$ into the wave spatial derivative:

$$ \frac{d^2 \psi}{dx^2} + \frac{2m}{\hbar^2}(E - V(x))\psi = 0 $$

This is the 1D Time-Independent Schrödinger Equation. Note: $\psi(x)$ itself is complex and unobservable, but $|\psi(x)|^2$ is the probability density function (Born interpretation).

3.5 Particle in a 1D Box

Consider a particle of mass $m$ strictly confined within an infinite, rigid, 1D potential well of width $L$. Inside the box ($0 < x < L$), $V(x)=0$. Outside, $V(x)=\infty$. The particle cannot exist outside, so the wavefunction must vanish at and beyond the boundaries: $\psi(0)=\psi(L)=0$.

Solving the differential equation inside the force-free box:

$$ \frac{d^2 \psi}{dx^2} + k^2\psi = 0 \quad \left(\text{where } k = \frac{\sqrt{2mE}}{\hbar}\right) $$

General solution: $\psi(x) = A \sin(kx) + B \cos(kx)$

Apply Boundary 1 ($x=0$): $\psi(0) = A(0) + B(1) = 0 \implies B = 0$.

Apply Boundary 2 ($x=L$): $\psi(L) = A \sin(kL) = 0$. For non-trivial solutions ($A \neq 0$), $kL = n\pi \implies k = \frac{n\pi}{L}$.

Normalized Spatial Wavefunction: By enforcing $\int_{0}^{L} |\psi|^2 dx = 1$, we find $A = \sqrt{2/L}$.

$$ \psi_n(x) = \sqrt{\frac{2}{L}} \sin\left(\frac{n\pi x}{L}\right) $$

Quantized Energy Eigenvalues: Substituting $k$ back into the energy algebraic definition ($E = \hbar^2 k^2 / 2m$):

$$ E_n = \frac{n^2 h^2}{8mL^2} \quad (n = 1, 2, 3...) $$

Note: $n=0$ is strictly forbidden because it yields $\psi(x)=0$ everywhere (no particle exists). Thus, a confined particle can never have zero kinetic energy (Zero Point Energy).

Module 4: Wave Optics

This module analyzes light interactions derived from the principles of superposition, specifically covering the phenomena of interference (superposition of finite coherent wave trains) and diffraction (superposition of infinite secondary wavelets from a single wavefront).

4.1 Interference in Thin Films

Interference occurs when a wavefront of light is split into two components by reflecting off the upper and lower boundaries of a microscopic transparent thin film (like an oil slick on water or a soap bubble). These two reflected waves travel different optical paths and subsequently recombine, phase-shifting to constructively or destructively merge.

Conditions for Interference in Reflected Light:

Path difference for reflected light from a film of strictly uniform thickness $t$ and refractive index $\mu$, with an angle of refraction $r$:

$$ \Delta = 2\mu t \cos(r) \pm \frac{\lambda}{2} $$

The crucial $\pm \lambda/2$ term accounts for Stoke's Phase Shift (an abrupt phase change of $\pi$ occurring upon reflection from a more optically dense medium boundary). For normal incidence, $r = 0^\circ \implies \cos(0) = 1$.

  • Constructive Interference (Bright/Max Intensity): $ 2\mu t \cos r = (2n-1)\frac{\lambda}{2} $ or $ (2n+1)\frac{\lambda}{2} $ where $n=1,2,3...$
  • Destructive Interference (Dark/Min Intensity): $ 2\mu t \cos r = n\lambda $ where $n=0,1,2,3...$

4.2 Newton's Rings

Newton's Rings describe circular, concentric interference fringes formed between a plano-convex lens of large focal length and an optically flat glass plate, practically entrapping a wedge-shaped air film of radially varying thickness.

[Diagram Placeholder: Shutterstock - Newton's Rings Concentric Bright and Dark Fringes Apparatus]

Derivation of Ring Diameter for Air Film:

Film thickness $t$ at a small radial distance $r$ from the contact center: $2Rt = r^2$, so $t = \frac{r^2}{2R}$ where $R$ is the lens radius of curvature.

For an air film ($\mu=1$) viewed normally in reflected light, the condition for a completely destructive dark ring is $2t = n\lambda \implies 2 \left(\frac{r^2}{2R}\right) = n\lambda$.

$$ r_n^2 = n\lambda R \implies D_n^2 = (2r_n)^2 = 4n\lambda R $$ $$ D_n = \sqrt{4n\lambda R} \propto \sqrt{n} $$

Conclusion: The diameters of dark rings are directly proportional to the square root of natural numbers. The diameters of bright rings ($D_n^2 = 2(2n-1)\lambda R$) are proportional to the square root of odd natural numbers.

4.3 Fraunhofer Diffraction at a Single Slit

Diffraction is the bending of light around sharp corners or obstacles, encroaching into geometrical shadow regions. In the Fraunhofer class, both the light source and screen are effectively strictly placed at infinity (achieved experimentally using convex lenses).

When planar monochromatic wavefronts pass through a narrow slit of width $e$, they produce a broad primary central maximum symmetrically surrounded by alternating, decreasing secondary maxima and minima on a distant focal plane.

Analytical Treatment: path difference between extreme wavelets originating from slit edges: $ \Delta x = e \sin\theta $.

Corresponding phase difference: $ 2\alpha = \frac{2\pi}{\lambda} (e \sin\theta) $.

By integrating the contributions of infinite infinitesimal slit elements, the Resultant Intensity $I$ is:

$$ I = I_0 \left( \frac{\sin\alpha}{\alpha} \right)^2 \quad \text{where } \alpha = \frac{\pi e \sin\theta}{\lambda} $$
  • Principal Maximum: As $\theta \to 0$, $\alpha \to 0$, and $\frac{\sin\alpha}{\alpha} \to 1 \implies I = I_0$ (highest uniform intensity).
  • Minima Positions: Occur when $\sin\alpha = 0$ (but $\alpha \neq 0$), so $\alpha = \pm n\pi$. This yields the general minima condition: $e \sin\theta = \pm n\lambda$ ($n=1,2,3...$).

4.4 Resolving Power of Diffraction Grating

Lord Rayleigh's criterion fundamentally states that two closely spaced spectral lines of distinct wavelengths $\lambda$ and $\lambda + d\lambda$ are "just resolved" if the principal maximum of one line exactly geometrically coincides with the first adjacent minimum of the other line.

Resolving Power (RP) mathematically quantifies an optical instrument's absolute ability to visibly separate adjacent spectral lines:

$$ RP = \frac{\lambda}{d\lambda} = nN $$

Where $n$ represents the specific order of the spectrum being observed, and $N$ is the total discrete number of illuminated ruled lines on the entire grating surface. Thus, larger gratings observing higher orders intrinsically provide higher resolution.

5.1 Fundamentals of LASER

LASER stands for Light Amplification by Stimulated Emission of Radiation. The core interactions of radiation with atomic matter include:

  • Induced Absorption: A ground state atom absorbs a photon ($E = h\nu$) and leaps to an excited state. Rate $\propto$ energy density and number of ground atoms.
  • Spontaneous Emission: An excited atom naturally decays to the ground state after its incredibly brief lifetime ($\sim 10^{-8} \text{ s}$), emitting a photon randomly in any direction (incoherent light).
  • Stimulated Emission: An incoming resonant photon induces an already-excited atom to decay, emitting a second photon identical in phase, wavelength, polarization, and direction to the incident triggering photon (making the emitted light fiercely coherent).

5.2 Population Inversion & Pumping

For LASER action (net light amplification), the rate of stimulated emission must critically exceed absorption. This physically requires more atoms in the excited state ($N_2$) than the ground state ($N_1$), an unnatural non-equilibrium condition termed Population Inversion.

It is actively achieved via "Pumping" mechanisms that continuously inject energy into the medium (e.g., optical pumping with flashlamps, electrical discharge, chemical reactions).

5.3 Types of Lasers

  • Ruby Laser (3-Level System): A cylindrical crystal of $Al_2O_3$ heavily doped with $Cr^{3+}$ ions (the active centers). It is optically pumped via a surrounding intensely bright Xenon flashlamp. As it requires depopulating the massive dense ground state, it requires massive pump power, limits efficiency, and yields only pulsed (non-continuous) red laser output ($694.3 \text{ nm}$).
  • He-Ne Laser (4-Level System): A gas discharge mixture (10 parts Helium: 1 part Neon). Helium efficiently absorbs electrical discharge energy and resonantly transfers it via inelastic collisions to Neon, causing massive population inversion in Ne. Because the lower lasing level continuously rapidly empties into the ground state, it elegantly ensures stable, continuous wave (CW) red emission ($632.8 \text{ nm}$).

5.4 Fibre Optics Principles

Optical fibers strictly guide light over massive distances relying on Total Internal Reflection (TIR) inside a dense optical core ($n_1$), continuously guarded by a distinctly lower refractive index cladding layer ($n_2$).

Acceptance Angle ($\theta_a$) and Numerical Aperture (NA):

The acceptance angle is the maximum boundary cone angle within which focused incident light will securely undergo TIR exclusively inside the fiber.

$$ NA = \sin\theta_a = \sqrt{n_{core}^2 - n_{cladding}^2} $$

Fractional Refractive Index Change ($\Delta$): Defines the relative index step:

$$ \Delta = \frac{n_{core} - n_{cladding}}{n_{core}} \implies NA = n_{core}\sqrt{2\Delta} $$

5.5 Types of Fibers & Dispersion

  • Step-Index Fiber: Strict interface boundary where the index drops rigidly and instantly from core to cladding. Causes severely high intermodal dispersion (different rays take different zigzag path lengths, blurring digital pulses).
  • Graded-Index (GRIN) Fiber: Core index precisely varies parabolically, highest at the center axis and decreasing toward the cladding boundary. Photons taking longer, curving outer paths physically travel faster through the lower index material, fundamentally equalizing travel times and drastically limiting dispersion.
