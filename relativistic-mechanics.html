Relativistic Mechanics

Understanding the physics of objects moving near the speed of light. Frames of reference, Lorentz transformations, length contraction, time dilation, and the equivalence of mass and energy.

Theoretical Framework

1. Frames of Reference

A frame of reference is a coordinate system consisting of coordinate axes (X, Y, Z) and a clock to measure time, used to describe the position and motion of an object.

Inertial Frames

An inertial frame of reference is one in which Newton's first law holds trueâ€”meaning an isolated object is either at rest or moving at a constant velocity. All inertial frames are in uniform translation motion with respect to one another.

Non-Inertial Frames

A non-inertial frame is a frame that is accelerating (either changing speed or direction) with respect to an inertial frame. Newton's laws appear violated here unless we introduce fictitious forces (like the centrifugal force).

2. Lorentz Transformations

The Galilean Transformation equations relate the coordinates of a particle in two inertial frames moving relative to each other at a constant speed \( v \) (where \( v \ll c \)). They assume time is absolute (\( t' = t \)).

However, the Michelson-Morley experiment and Einstein's postulates of Special Relativity (constancy of the speed of light) showed that Galilean transformations fail at high velocities. Lorentz proposed new transformations.

Derivation of Lorentz Transformation Equations

Consider two inertial frames \( S \) and \( S' \). Let \( S' \) move with velocity \( v \) along the positive x-axis relative to \( S \). At \( t = t' = 0 \), their origins coincide. A light pulse is emitted from the origin. The position of the wavefront in both frames is given by:

$$ x^2 + y^2 + z^2 = c^2 t^2 \quad \text{(in frame } S \text{)} $$ $$ {x'}^2 + {y'}^2 + {z'}^2 = c^2 {t'}^2 \quad \text{(in frame } S' \text{)} $$

Based on the linear relationship and symmetry, we assume a transformation form: \( x' = \gamma (x - vt) \) and \( x = \gamma (x' + vt') \), where \( \gamma \) is a constant.

Substituting into the wavefront equations and solving for \( \gamma \), we find:

$$ \gamma = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1 - \frac{v^2}{c^2}}} $$

The complete set of Lorentz Transformation Equations is:

$$ x' = \frac{x - vt}{\sqrt{1 - v^2/c^2}} $$ $$ y' = y $$ $$ z' = z $$ $$ t' = \frac{t - \frac{vx}{c^2}}{\sqrt{1 - v^2/c^2}} $$

3. Consequences: Length Contraction & Time Dilation

Length Contraction

An object moving relative to an observer appears shortened along the direction of motion. Let \( L_0 = x_2' - x_1' \) be proper length in frame \( S' \).

$$ L_0 = \frac{x_2 - vt}{\sqrt{1 - v^2/c^2}} - \frac{x_1 - vt}{\sqrt{1 - v^2/c^2}} = \frac{x_2 - x_1}{\sqrt{1 - v^2/c^2}} = \frac{L}{\sqrt{1 - v^2/c^2}} $$ $$ \implies L = L_0 \sqrt{1 - \frac{v^2}{c^2}} $$

Time Dilation

A moving clock ticks slower relative to a stationary observer. Let a process occur at a fixed position \( x' \) in frame \( S' \), taking time \(\Delta t' = t_2' - t_1'\) (proper time \(\Delta t_0\)).

$$ \Delta t = t_2 - t_1 = \frac{t_2' + v x'/c^2}{\sqrt{1-v^2/c^2}} - \frac{t_1' + v x'/c^2}{\sqrt{1-v^2/c^2}} = \frac{t_2' - t_1'}{\sqrt{1-v^2/c^2}} $$ $$ \implies \Delta t = \frac{\Delta t_0}{\sqrt{1 - \frac{v^2}{c^2}}} $$

4. Mass-Energy Equivalence

Einstein demonstrated that mass is a form of energy. He derived the famous relationship between mass \( m \) and energy \( E \).

Starting with relativisitic momentum \( p = \gamma m_0 v \), the rate of work done on a particle increases its kinetic energy \( K \):

$$ dK = F \cdot dx = \left(\frac{dp}{dt}\right) dx = v \cdot dp $$

Integrating by parts and substituting relativistic mass yields the total energy (Rest Energy + Kinetic Energy):

$$ E = m_0 c^2 + K = m c^2 $$

Where \( m_0 \) is rest mass, and \( m = \frac{m_0}{\sqrt{1-v^2/c^2}} \) is relativistic mass.

Solved Examples

5. Numerical Problems (AKTU Pattern)

Q1: Time Dilation

Problem: A clock keeps correct time. With what speed should it be moved relative to an observer so that it may seem to lose 1 minute in 24 hours?

Solution:

Proper time \(\Delta t_0 = (24 \times 60) - 1 = 1439\) minutes.

Observed time \(\Delta t = 24 \times 60 = 1440\) minutes.

$$ \Delta t = \frac{\Delta t_0}{\sqrt{1 - v^2/c^2}} $$ $$ 1440 = \frac{1439}{\sqrt{1 - v^2/c^2}} $$ $$ 1 - \frac{v^2}{c^2} = \left(\frac{1439}{1440}\right)^2 \approx 0.9986 $$ $$ \frac{v^2}{c^2} = 0.0014 \implies v = \sqrt{0.0014} \cdot c = 0.0374c $$

Answer: The clock must move at approximately \( 1.12 \times 10^7 \) m/s.

Q2: Mass-Energy Equivalence

Problem: Calculate the amount of work required to accelerate an electron from a velocity of \(0.6c\) to \(0.8c\). (Rest mass of electron \(m_0 = 9.1 \times 10^{-31}\) kg)

Solution:

Work done = Change in kinetic energy = \( \Delta K = K_2 - K_1 \)

$$ K = (\gamma - 1) m_0 c^2 $$ $$ \gamma_1 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1 - 0.6^2}} = 1.25 $$ $$ \gamma_2 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1 - 0.8^2}} = 1.667 $$ $$ \Delta K = (\gamma_2 - \gamma_1) m_0 c^2 = (1.667 - 1.25) \times 9.1 \times 10^{-31} \times (3 \times 10^8)^2 $$ $$ \Delta K = 0.417 \times 9.1 \times 10^{-31} \times 9 \times 10^{16} = 3.41 \times 10^{-14} \text{ Joules} $$

Answer: The work required is \( 3.41 \times 10^{-14} \) Joules (or approx 0.213 MeV).
