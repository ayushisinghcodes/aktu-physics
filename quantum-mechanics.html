Unit II

Quantum Mechanics

Delving into the microscopic world. Discover the wave-particle duality, uncertainty principle, and the fundamental equations governing quantum systems.

Theoretical Framework

1. de-Broglie Matter Waves

In 1924, Louis de Broglie hypothesized that since light (radiation) exhibits both wave and particle nature, moving material particles (like electrons) should also exhibit wave-like properties. The wave associated with a moving particle is called a matter wave or de-Broglie wave.

The wavelength \( \lambda \) of the matter wave is inversely proportional to its momentum \( p \):

$$ \lambda = \frac{h}{p} = \frac{h}{mv} $$

Where \( h \) is Planck's constant (\(6.626 \times 10^{-34} \text{ J s}\)).

In terms of Kinetic Energy \( K \):

$$ \lambda = \frac{h}{\sqrt{2mK}} $$
Theoretical Framework Mathematical Derivations

2. Heisenberg's Uncertainty Principle

It is impossible to determine simultaneously and accurately both the position and momentum of a moving microscopic particle. If \( \Delta x \) is the uncertainty in position and \( \Delta p \) is the uncertainty in momentum, their product must be greater than or equal to a minimum value.

$$ \Delta x \cdot \Delta p \ge \frac{\hbar}{2} $$

Where \( \hbar = \frac{h}{2\pi} \) is the reduced Planck constant.

Other forms of the principle include Energy-Time uncertainty:

$$ \Delta E \cdot \Delta t \ge \frac{\hbar}{2} $$

Mathematical Derivations

3. Schrödinger Wave Equations

The Schrödinger equation describes how the quantum state of a physical system changes over time. It is the quantum equivalent of Newton's laws.

Time-Dependent Form

For a non-relativistic particle of mass \( m \) moving in a potential \( V(x,t) \) in 1D:

$$ i\hbar \frac{\partial \Psi(x,t)}{\partial t} = -\frac{\hbar^2}{2m} \frac{\partial^2 \Psi(x,t)}{\partial x^2} + V(x,t)\Psi(x,t) $$

Time-Independent Form

If the potential \( V \) is independent of time, we can use separation of variables \( \Psi(x,t) = \psi(x) e^{-iEt/\hbar} \) to isolate the spatial part.

$$ \frac{d^2 \psi(x)}{dx^2} + \frac{2m}{\hbar^2} (E - V(x)) \psi(x) = 0 $$

Where \( E \) is the total energy of the system.

4. Particle in a 1D Box (Infinite Potential Well)

Consider a particle of mass \( m \) trapped inside a 1D box of length \( L \) (from \( x=0 \) to \( x=L \)). Inside the box, the potential \( V(x) = 0 \). Outside the box, \( V(x) = \infty \), meaning the particle cannot escape.

Wavefunction Derivation

Inside the box, the time-independent Schrödinger equation becomes:

$$ \frac{d^2 \psi}{dx^2} + \frac{2mE}{\hbar^2} \psi = 0 \implies \frac{d^2 \psi}{dx^2} + k^2 \psi = 0 $$

Where \( k = \frac{\sqrt{2mE}}{\hbar} \)

The general solution is \( \psi(x) = A \sin(kx) + B \cos(kx) \).

Applying boundary conditions: \( \psi(0) = 0 \) and \( \psi(L) = 0 \).

1. \( \psi(0) = B = 0 \implies \psi(x) = A \sin(kx) \)

2. \( \psi(L) = A \sin(kL) = 0 \).

Since \( A \neq 0 \) (particle must exist), \( \sin(kL) = 0 \implies kL = n\pi \) where \( n = 1, 2, 3, ... \)

Quantized Energy Levels

Substituting \( k = \frac{n\pi}{L} \) back into the expression for \( k \):

$$ E_n = \frac{n^2 h^2}{8mL^2} $$

The energies are quantized. The ground state energy (\( n=1 \)) is not zero, demonstrating the zero-point energy.

Solved Examples

5. Numerical Problems (AKTU Pattern)

Q1: de-Broglie Wavelength

Problem: Calculate the de-Broglie wavelength of an electron accelerated through a potential difference of 54 Volts.

Solution:

When an electron is accelerated through a potential \(V\), its kinetic energy \(K = eV\).

$$ \lambda = \frac{h}{\sqrt{2m_e eV}} $$

For an electron, evaluating constants yields a direct formula: \( \lambda (\text{in \AA}) = \frac{12.27}{\sqrt{V}} \)

$$ \lambda = \frac{12.27}{\sqrt{54}} \approx \frac{12.27}{7.348} \approx 1.67 \text{ \AA} $$

Answer: The wavelength is approximately \(1.67 \text{ \AA}\).

Q2: Particle in a Box

Problem: An electron is confined to a 1D box of width \( 1 \text{ \AA} \). Calculate the energy in eV required to excite the electron from its ground state to the first excited state.

Solution:

Width \(L = 10^{-10}\) m. Mass \(m_e = 9.1 \times 10^{-31}\) kg. Ground state \(n_1=1\), First excited state \(n_2=2\).

$$ E_n = n^2 \frac{h^2}{8mL^2} $$ $$ \Delta E = E_2 - E_1 = (2^2 - 1^2) \frac{h^2}{8mL^2} = 3 \frac{h^2}{8mL^2} $$ $$ E_1 = \frac{(6.626 \times 10^{-34})^2}{8 \times 9.1 \times 10^{-31} \times (10^{-10})^2} \text{ Joules} = 6.03 \times 10^{-18} \text{ J} $$

Convert \(E_1\) to eV: \( \frac{6.03 \times 10^{-18}}{1.6 \times 10^{-19}} \approx 37.6 \text{ eV} \)

$$ \Delta E = 3 \times 37.6 \approx 112.8 \text{ eV} $$

Answer: The required energy is approximately \(112.8\) eV.
